General Settings

Core configuration options that control how the repricing algorithm behaves.

Toggle Switch enabled - Master On/Off Switch

What It Is

The master control that turns repricing on or off for this specific product and vendor combination.

What It Does

When ON (enabled): The V2 algorithm will actively reprice this product according to all the other settings you've configured.

When OFF (disabled): The algorithm completely ignores this product. Your current price remains unchanged until you manually update it or turn this back on.

When to Use It

  • Turn ON when you want automated competitive repricing for this product
  • Turn OFF temporarily if you're running a manual promotion or special pricing
  • Turn OFF if you're out of stock and don't want price changes
  • Turn OFF while testing other settings (prevents unwanted price changes)
Important: When you disable this, all the other settings remain saved but inactive. Re-enabling immediately restarts repricing with your existing configuration.
Example: You're launching a Black Friday sale with fixed pricing. Turn OFF "enabled" for the sale duration, then turn it back ON when you want to resume competitive repricing.

Number execution_priority - Processing Order

What It Is

A number that determines the order in which products are repriced when the algorithm runs.

What It Does

Lower numbers = higher priority = repriced first. Products with priority 0 are processed before priority 1, which are processed before priority 2, etc.

When to Use It

  • Priority 0: Your best-selling products, flagship items, high-margin products
  • Priority 1-3: Standard inventory, moderate sellers
  • Priority 4+: Slow-moving items, low-margin products, clearance items

Common Values

Priority Use Case Processing Speed
0 Critical products First to update
1 High-value items Second wave
2-5 Standard inventory Middle of queue
6+ Low priority items Last to update
Pro Tip: If the algorithm runs out of time or resources, lower priority products might not get repriced in that run. They'll be picked up in the next cycle.

Dropdown up_down - Price Direction Control

What It Is

Controls whether your prices can move up, down, or both directions based on market conditions.

What It Does

  • UP/DOWN: Prices can move in any direction based on competition (most flexible)
  • UP: Prices can only increase, never decrease (conservative, protects margins)
  • DOWN: Prices can only decrease, never increase (aggressive competition)

When to Use Each Option

UP/DOWN (Recommended)

Best for: Most situations

  • Standard competitive pricing
  • Products with stable demand
  • Markets with changing competition
  • When you trust the algorithm fully

UP Only

Best for: Premium positioning

  • High-margin luxury products
  • Limited availability items
  • When you're already lowest price
  • Protecting profit margins strictly

DOWN Only

Best for: Aggressive market capture

  • Clearance items you need to move
  • Products with expiration dates
  • When you're currently overpriced
  • Gaining market share (accepting lower margins)
Warning: "DOWN Only" can erode your margins if not monitored. Always set a floor_price when using this option!
Example Decision Tree:
Do you need maximum flexibility? → Use UP/DOWN
Are margins more important than market share? → Use UP
Do you need to clear inventory quickly? → Use DOWN

Dropdown handling_time_group - Shipping Speed Filter

What It Is

Filters which competitors you compare against based on how fast they can ship the product.

What It Does

The algorithm only looks at competitors whose shipping speed matches or is slower than your selection:

Option Shipping Time Who You Compete With
ALL Any speed Everyone (regardless of shipping time)
FAST_SHIPPING 1-2 days Only sellers with 1-2 day handling
STOCKED 1-5 days Sellers with standard stocked inventory
LONG_HANDLING 6+ days Sellers with slower fulfillment

When to Use Each Option

  • ALL: Most common. Use when shipping speed isn't a differentiator for this product
  • FAST_SHIPPING: Your warehouse stocks the item and ships quickly. Don't compete with drop-shippers who take weeks
  • STOCKED: Standard inventory items that ship within a week
  • LONG_HANDLING: Made-to-order or special order items with longer lead times
Business Logic: If you can ship in 2 days, you shouldn't have to match prices from sellers who take 3 weeks. Customers pay more for faster shipping, so this filter ensures fair comparison.
Example: You stock dental gloves and ship same-day. Set to FAST_SHIPPING so you only compete with other fast shippers. Don't race to the bottom against drop-shippers with 14-day handling times.

Dropdown badge_indicator - Badge Seller Filter

What It Is

Controls whether you compete with all sellers or only with "badge" sellers (verified/trusted sellers on the platform).

What It Does

Option Behavior Best For
ALL Compete with everyone (badge and non-badge sellers) Maximum market competition, commodity products
BADGE Only compete with verified badge sellers Premium positioning, quality differentiation

When to Use Each Option

Use ALL When:
  • You want maximum competitiveness
  • The product is a commodity (gloves, masks)
  • Badge status doesn't matter to buyers
  • You need to capture market share aggressively
Use BADGE When:
  • You have a badge and want premium positioning
  • Quality/trust matters to your buyers
  • You don't want to compete with unknown sellers
  • Protecting margins is more important than volume
Important: This interacts with the badge percentage settings (reprice_up_badge_percentage, reprice_down_badge_percentage). If set to BADGE, those percentage fields become active.
Real-World Example: You sell premium dental chairs. Set to BADGE so you only compete with other established, trusted sellers. Don't race to the bottom against new sellers with questionable quality.

Dropdown price_strategy - How to Compare Prices

What It Is

Determines what type of price the algorithm uses when comparing against competitors.

What It Does

Strategy Compares Based On Best For
UNIT Price per single item Products sold individually (most common)
TOTAL Total price including all fees Products where total cost matters more than unit price
BUY_BOX Who wins the "Buy Box" (featured seller position) Highly competitive marketplaces like Amazon

Detailed Strategy Explanations

UNIT Strategy (Default)

Compares per-unit prices. If competitors sell at $10/item, you'll price relative to that $10.

Example: Dental masks sold individually. Competitor A: $0.50/mask, Competitor B: $0.55/mask. You reprice based on these unit prices.

TOTAL Strategy

Compares total order cost including shipping, handling, and any other fees.

Example: Large equipment. Competitor A: $1000 + $150 shipping = $1150 total. Competitor B: $1050 + $75 shipping = $1125 total. You compete on the $1125 total, not the $1000 unit price.

BUY_BOX Strategy

Advanced strategy that aims to win the featured "Buy Box" position on the marketplace. Uses complex algorithms including price, shipping, seller rating, and stock availability.

Warning: BUY_BOX strategy is advanced. It considers factors beyond just price. Only use if you understand buy box algorithms.

When to Use Each Strategy

  • UNIT: 90% of products. Standard competitive repricing.
  • TOTAL: Heavy/bulky items where shipping varies significantly between sellers.
  • BUY_BOX: High-competition marketplaces where featured position drives most sales.

Percentage Settings

Control how aggressively you compete by setting percentage thresholds for price adjustments.

Number reprice_up_percentage - How Much to Raise Prices

What It Is

The percentage by which you'll raise your price when competitors' prices go up.

What It Does

When the market moves up and you have room to increase prices without losing competitiveness, this controls how much you increase.

Special value: -1 means "no limit" - follow the market up as high as needed (up to max_price)

Common Values

Value Behavior Use Case
-1 No limit (follow market) Standard setting - let market determine price
1-3% Conservative increases Price-sensitive customers, maintain volume
5-10% Moderate increases Balance margin improvement with volume
10%+ Aggressive increases Low competition, unique products

When to Use It

  • -1 (Recommended default): Let the market determine pricing, trust the algorithm
  • Small percentages (1-3%): When you're worried about losing customers to price increases
  • Larger percentages (5-10%): When you're confident in your value proposition
Example: You're selling at $100. Competitors raise to $110. With reprice_up_percentage = 5%, you'll go to $105 (not all the way to $110). With -1, you'd match the $110.
Pro Tip: Use -1 for most products. Only set specific percentages when you have a strategic reason to limit price increases.

Number reprice_down_percentage - How Much to Undercut Competitors

What It Is

The percentage by which you'll undercut the lowest competitor price to win sales.

What It Does

When competitors lower prices, this determines how much cheaper you'll go to stay competitive.

Special value: -1 means "match exactly" - don't undercut, just match their price

Common Values

Value Behavior Use Case
-1 Match competitor exactly Parity pricing, shared buy box
1-2% Slightly undercut Win sales without sacrificing margin
3-5% Moderate undercut Standard competitive positioning
5-10% Aggressive undercut Market share grab, clear inventory
10%+ Very aggressive Clearance, expiring products

When to Use Different Percentages

Conservative (1-2%)
  • High-margin products
  • Unique value proposition
  • Loyal customer base
  • Limited competition
Aggressive (5-10%+)
  • Commodity products
  • High competition
  • Need quick inventory turnover
  • Market share more important than margin
Critical Warning: Always set a floor_price when using aggressive down percentages! Without it, a price war could push you below cost.
Example: Competitor price: $100. With reprice_down_percentage = 3%, you'll set $97. With 5%, you'll set $95. With -1, you'll match at $100.

Number reprice_up_badge_percentage - Raise Against Badge Sellers

What It Is

How much you raise prices specifically when competing with badge (verified) sellers.

What It Does

Only active when badge_indicator = BADGE. Controls price increases when badge seller prices go up.

Special value: -1 means no limit - follow badge sellers up without restriction

When to Use It

  • Only matters if badge_indicator is set to BADGE
  • Usually set to -1 to follow badge seller pricing
  • Set specific percentage if you want to stay slightly below badge sellers even when they raise prices
Typical Setting: Most users set this to -1 (no limit) and let the market determine pricing among badge sellers.

Number reprice_down_badge_percentage - Undercut Badge Sellers

What It Is

How much you undercut specifically when competing with badge (verified) sellers.

What It Does

Only active when badge_indicator = BADGE. Controls how much cheaper you price compared to other badge sellers.

Common Values

Value Strategy
-1 Match badge sellers exactly (premium positioning)
1-2% Slightly cheaper than badge sellers (competitive)
3-5% Noticeably cheaper while maintaining quality perception
Example Strategy: Badge seller at $100. With reprice_down_badge_percentage = 2%, you go to $98. You're still in the "premium badge seller" tier but slightly more attractive on price.

Price Boundaries

Set hard limits on how low or high your prices can go. Critical for protecting margins and brand positioning.

Number floor_price - Absolute Minimum Price

What It Is

The lowest price you'll ever accept for this product. A safety net that prevents the algorithm from destroying your margins.

What It Does

No matter what competitors do, the algorithm will never price below this amount. If the calculated competitive price is lower than floor_price, you stay at floor_price.

How to Calculate Floor Price

Formula Approach

Floor Price = (Cost × 1.10) + Shipping + Fees

Where:

  • Cost: What you pay for the product
  • 1.10: Minimum 10% margin (adjust based on business needs)
  • Shipping: Your average shipping cost for this item
  • Fees: Marketplace fees, payment processing, etc.
Real Example:
Product cost: $50
Desired minimum margin: 15% → $50 × 1.15 = $57.50
Shipping: $5
Marketplace fees: 10% of sale price → ~$6.25
Floor Price: $68.75 (round to $69)

When to Set Floor Price

  • Always - This is your most important protection
  • Set it based on your true cost structure, not competitor pricing
  • Include ALL costs (product, shipping, fees, overhead)
  • Add your minimum acceptable margin percentage
Critical: Never set floor_price to 0 unless you deliberately want no floor. A $0 floor means the algorithm could theoretically price your product at $0.01. Always set a meaningful floor based on your costs.
Market Alert: If you frequently hit your floor price, it means:
  • The market price is below your costs (unprofitable market)
  • You need to find cheaper suppliers
  • This product might not be worth selling
  • Consider exiting this market or finding differentiation

Number max_price - Absolute Maximum Price

What It Is

The highest price you'll ever charge for this product. A ceiling that prevents absurd pricing.

What It Does

Even if all competitors raise their prices, the algorithm will never price above this amount. Prevents accidentally pricing yourself out of the market.

Common Values

Setting Value Use Case
MSRP Manufacturer suggested price Don't exceed retail pricing guidelines
150% of normal price Your median price × 1.5 Allow market fluctuations but cap extremes
200% of cost Your cost × 2 Maximum margin you're comfortable with
99999999.99 (default) Essentially unlimited Trust market pricing completely

When to Set Max Price

  • MSRP restrictions: Manufacturer requires you don't exceed certain price
  • Brand protection: Don't want to be seen as price gouging
  • Customer trust: Maintain reasonable pricing reputation
  • Market sanity check: Prevent algorithm errors from pricing at $10,000
Example: You sell dental chairs. Normal price: $800-$1000. Set max_price = $1500 to prevent pricing at $3000 if there's a temporary supply shortage. You'd rather sell out at $1500 than damage your brand reputation.
Default Behavior: The migration sets this to 99999999.99 (essentially unlimited). For most products, you'll want to set a realistic maximum based on your market.

Vendor Competition Settings

Control which sellers you compete with and which you ignore. Advanced market positioning controls.

Comma-Separated sister_vendor_ids - Your Other Brands

What It Is

A list of vendor IDs that belong to your company but operate under different brand names.

What It Does

The algorithm will ignore these vendors when calculating competitive pricing. You don't compete against yourself.

Format

Enter vendor IDs separated by commas: 12345,67890,24680

When to Use It

  • You operate multiple storefronts or brands
  • You have a premium brand and a budget brand
  • You white-label the same product under different names
  • Prevent cannibalization between your own channels
Example: Your company runs "Premium Dental Supply" (vendor 20722) and "Budget Dental Warehouse" (vendor 20755). When repricing Premium Dental, enter 20755 in sister_vendor_ids so you don't compete with your own budget brand.
Business Case: Allows you to maintain different market positions (premium vs budget) without the algorithm forcing a race to the bottom between your own brands.

Comma-Separated exclude_vendors - Don't Compete With These

What It Is

A list of specific vendor IDs you want to ignore when repricing. The algorithm acts as if they don't exist.

What It Does

These vendors are completely ignored during price calculations. Their prices don't influence your repricing at all.

Format

Enter vendor IDs separated by commas: 11111,22222,33333

When to Use It

  • Known bad actors: Sellers who use fake pricing or bait-and-switch tactics
  • Counterfeit sellers: Vendors selling knock-offs at unrealistic prices
  • Strategic exclusion: Major competitor you deliberately ignore
  • Quality difference: Sellers offering inferior versions of the product
Real-World Example: Vendor 99999 consistently lists products at $1 but never actually has stock (bait pricing). Add 99999 to exclude_vendors so their fake prices don't crash your pricing.
Use Carefully: Excluding vendors means you might not see their legitimate competitive moves. Only exclude when you have a strong strategic reason.

Text inactive_vendor_id - Mark Vendors as Inactive

What It Is

Vendor IDs that should be treated as inactive or unavailable for this product.

What It Does

Similar to exclude_vendors but specifically for vendors who are temporarily out of stock or inactive.

When to Use It

  • Competitor is temporarily out of stock
  • Vendor is on vacation/hiatus
  • Seasonal availability (they only sell certain times of year)
Difference from exclude_vendors: Use inactive_vendor_id for temporary situations. Use exclude_vendors for permanent strategic exclusions.

Checkbox compete_with_all_vendors - Ignore All Filters

What It Is

Override switch that makes the algorithm compete with every vendor, ignoring all your exclusion settings.

What It Does

When ON: Ignores sister_vendor_ids, exclude_vendors, and inactive_vendor_id. Competes with literally everyone.

When OFF: Respects all your vendor exclusion settings (normal behavior).

When to Use It

  • Turn ON: Testing or emergency situations where you need maximum competitiveness
  • Keep OFF: Normal operations (recommended 99% of the time)
Warning: Turning this ON overrides all your carefully configured vendor exclusions. Only use temporarily or in special circumstances.

Inventory & Threshold Settings

Control how inventory levels affect competitive behavior. Advanced features for stock-aware repricing.

Number inventory_competition_threshold - Minimum Stock to Compete

What It Is

The minimum inventory quantity a competitor must have before you consider them a real threat.

What It Does

The algorithm ignores competitors who have fewer than this many units in stock. They're considered "not really available" for competitive purposes.

Common Values

Value Meaning Use Case
1 Any stock counts Default - compete with anyone who has the product
5-10 Meaningful inventory Ignore sellers with just a few units (might be clearing old stock)
25-50 Significant inventory Only compete with serious, well-stocked competitors
100+ Major stockist only Only compete with major distributors, ignore small sellers

When to Increase This Threshold

  • Bulk products: Items typically sold in large quantities
  • Avoid clearance pricing: Don't react to someone dumping 2 units
  • Stable market: Focus on major competitors with real inventory
Example: You sell dental gloves in boxes. Set threshold = 10. A competitor with 3 boxes (clearing old stock at fire-sale prices) won't trigger repricing. You only react to competitors with 10+ boxes who are seriously competing.
Business Logic: Prevents racing to the bottom because someone is dumping their last 2 units at cost. You compete with real competitors who have real inventory.

Number own_vendor_threshold - Your Minimum Stock Level

What It Is

The minimum inventory YOU must have before the repricing algorithm activates for this product.

What It Does

If your stock falls below this threshold, repricing stops. Your price stays at the last repriced amount until you restock.

Common Values

Value Behavior Use Case
1 Reprice if you have any stock Default - reprice as long as you can sell
5-10 Stop repricing when low Preserve last few units at current price
25+ Only reprice with healthy stock Prevent aggressive pricing when inventory is tight

When to Set Higher Threshold

  • Protect limited stock: When you have 5 units left, you don't want to price aggressively and sell out instantly
  • Restock planning: Maintain higher prices when you're low until new shipment arrives
  • Prevent stockouts: Slow down sales when inventory is tight
Example: Set own_vendor_threshold = 10. You have 150 units, repricing works normally. Stock drops to 8 units, repricing stops. Price stays where it is (probably higher than if you kept repricing down). You preserve those last 8 units for regular customers at decent margins.
Inventory Strategy: This is a "slow down when low" mechanism. Higher threshold = more conservative as you approach stockout. Lower threshold = keep competing even with minimal stock.

Boolean Flags - Special Behaviors

On/Off switches that control specific algorithm behaviors. Advanced features for special cases.

Checkbox suppress_price_break - Disable Quantity Pricing

What It Is

Controls whether the algorithm considers "price breaks" (bulk quantity discounts) when repricing.

What It Does

OFF (unchecked): Algorithm looks at price breaks (buy 10 get discount, buy 100 get bigger discount).

ON (checked): Algorithm ignores all price breaks and only considers base unit pricing.

When to Turn ON

  • Product doesn't have quantity discounts
  • You don't want to compete on bulk pricing
  • Price breaks are confusing your repricing
  • You only sell single units or fixed quantities

When to Keep OFF

  • Product commonly sold in bulk (boxes, cases, pallets)
  • Competitors offer quantity discounts
  • Your pricing strategy includes volume discounts
Example: You sell dental masks. Competitors offer: 1-9 masks = $1 each, 10-99 = $0.90, 100+ = $0.80. If suppress_price_break is OFF, algorithm considers all three tiers. If ON, it only looks at the $1 base price.

Checkbox suppress_price_break_if_Q1_not_updated - Conditional Price Break Disable

What It Is

Disables price break consideration if the first quantity tier (Q1) hasn't been updated recently.

What It Does

ON: If Q1 pricing is stale/old, ignore ALL price breaks and use base pricing only.

OFF: Use price breaks regardless of when they were last updated.

When to Use It

  • Competitors have outdated bulk pricing that distorts the market
  • You want to ensure only current, relevant price breaks influence repricing
  • Stale data is causing pricing errors
Technical Detail: Q1 = first quantity tier (usually 1-10 units). If this tier hasn't been updated in the system, it might be stale data, so this flag ignores it.

Checkbox compete_on_price_break_only - Bulk Pricing Only

What It Is

Opposite of suppress_price_break. Makes the algorithm ONLY look at bulk pricing, ignoring single-unit prices.

What It Does

ON: Algorithm only considers quantity discounts (Q2, Q3, etc.), ignores base unit price.

OFF: Algorithm considers all pricing including unit price (normal behavior).

When to Turn ON

  • Product is primarily sold in bulk (whole boxes, cases, pallets)
  • Single unit sales are rare or irrelevant
  • Your business model focuses on volume buyers
  • Unit price competition isn't meaningful
Example: You sell dental chairs. Nobody buys 1 chair - they buy 5+ for a clinic. Turn ON compete_on_price_break_only so you compete on the 5-chair pricing tier, not the meaningless single-chair price.
Mutual Exclusion: Don't turn ON both suppress_price_break AND compete_on_price_break_only. They contradict each other. Choose one strategy.

Checkbox compare_q2_with_q1 - Compare Quantity Tiers

What It Is

Makes the algorithm compare second quantity tier (Q2) pricing against first tier (Q1) pricing when repricing.

What It Does

ON: Algorithm ensures Q2 (bulk) pricing is actually cheaper than Q1 (small quantity) pricing. Maintains logical price break structure.

OFF: Q1 and Q2 are treated independently (normal behavior).

When to Turn ON

  • You want to ensure bulk discounts make sense (Q2 should be cheaper than Q1)
  • Prevent pricing errors where bulk is more expensive than single units
  • Maintain logical quantity discount structure
Example: Q1 (1-10 units) = $10 each. Q2 (11-50 units) = $9 each. With compare_q2_with_q1 = ON, algorithm ensures Q2 stays below Q1. If repricing would make Q2 = $11, it's rejected as illogical.

Checkbox floor_compete_with_next - Match Next Seller at Floor

What It Is

Special behavior when you hit your floor price. Instead of staying at floor, you match the next competitor above floor.

What It Does

OFF (normal): When calculated price would go below floor, you stay at floor_price exactly.

ON (competitive): When calculated price would go below floor, you instead match the next competitor who is above your floor.

When to Turn ON

  • You want to remain competitive even when you can't go lower
  • Matching next-best price is better than sitting at floor
  • You're willing to be slightly more expensive than lowest price if you can't afford to match it
Scenario:
Your floor_price: $50
Competitor A: $45 (below your floor)
Competitor B: $52 (above your floor)
Competitor C: $55

With floor_compete_with_next = OFF: You stay at $50 (your floor)
With floor_compete_with_next = ON: You match Competitor B at $52 (next above floor)
Business Logic: Sometimes being at floor looks suspicious to customers ("Why so cheap?"). Matching the next legitimate competitor maintains trust while still respecting your margin requirements.

Checkbox keep_position - Maintain Competitive Rank

What It Is

Maintains your position in the competitive ranking (e.g., always 2nd cheapest, always 3rd cheapest) rather than exact price matching.

What It Does

ON: Algorithm tries to keep you at the same rank position (e.g., if you're 2nd cheapest, stay 2nd cheapest even as prices change).

OFF: Normal price-based repricing (most common).

When to Turn ON

  • You want to maintain a consistent market position
  • "Always second cheapest" is your strategy
  • "Never lowest but always competitive" positioning
  • Avoiding price leadership (let others go first)
Example: Market has 5 sellers at $90, $95, $100, $105, $110. You're at $100 (3rd position). Tomorrow, prices shift to $85, $90, $95, $100, $105. With keep_position = ON, you move to $95 to maintain 3rd position. With OFF, you might stay at $100 or reprice based on percentages.
Advanced Feature: This is a sophisticated strategy. Most users should keep this OFF and use percentage-based repricing. Only enable if you have a specific positional strategy.

Price Break Dependencies

These three fields are mutually related:

Field Cannot Be Used With Why
suppress_price_break = ON compete_on_price_break_only = ON Contradictory: can't both suppress AND focus on price breaks
compete_on_price_break_only = ON suppress_price_break = ON Contradictory: must choose one strategy
Valid Combinations:
  • suppress_price_break = OFF, compete_on_price_break_only = OFF → Use all pricing (normal)
  • suppress_price_break = ON, compete_on_price_break_only = OFF → Ignore price breaks
  • suppress_price_break = OFF, compete_on_price_break_only = ON → Only use price breaks

Vendor Exclusion Dependencies

Override Behavior:
If compete_with_all_vendors = ON, it overrides:
  • sister_vendor_ids (ignored)
  • exclude_vendors (ignored)
  • inactive_vendor_id (ignored)
You'll compete with literally everyone, including your own brands and excluded vendors.

Floor Price Dependencies

Interaction Behavior
floor_price + floor_compete_with_next When floor is hit, match next competitor above floor (if enabled)
floor_price + reprice_down_percentage Floor overrides percentage. If calculation goes below floor, floor wins.
floor_price + max_price Must have floor_price < max_price. If equal, no room for repricing.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Learn from others' errors. These are the most frequent configuration mistakes and how to fix them.

Mistake #1: No Floor Price Set

Problem: Leaving floor_price at 0 or not setting it at all.

Why It's Bad: Algorithm could theoretically price your product at $0.01 in a race to the bottom.

Fix: Always calculate and set a meaningful floor: Cost + Fees + Minimum Margin

Example: Product costs $50, fees are $8, you need 10% margin minimum → Floor = $64

Mistake #2: Contradictory Price Break Settings

Problem: Setting both suppress_price_break = ON and compete_on_price_break_only = ON

Why It's Bad: These are opposites. Algorithm can't both ignore and focus on price breaks.

Fix: Choose one strategy:

  • Both OFF = normal (use all pricing)
  • suppress ON, compete OFF = ignore price breaks
  • suppress OFF, compete ON = only price breaks

Mistake #3: Aggressive Down Percentage Without Floor

Problem: Setting reprice_down_percentage = 10% but floor_price too low or at 0

Why It's Bad: You'll aggressively undercut until you hit an inadequate floor, destroying margins.

Fix: Before setting aggressive down percentages, ensure floor_price protects your margins.

Mistake #4: Wrong Badge Strategy for Your Situation

Problem: Premium seller using badge_indicator = ALL, or budget seller using BADGE

Why It's Bad: Premium seller competes with low-quality sellers, races to bottom. Budget seller ignores most of market.

Fix: Match strategy to positioning:

  • Premium/Quality focus → badge_indicator = BADGE
  • Volume/Price focus → badge_indicator = ALL

Mistake #5: Not Excluding Sister Vendors

Problem: Running multiple brands but not setting sister_vendor_ids

Why It's Bad: Your brands compete against each other, race to the bottom, cannibalize margins.

Fix: List all your other vendor IDs in sister_vendor_ids field for each brand.

Mistake #6: Forgetting to Enable After Configuration

Problem: Spending time configuring all settings but leaving enabled = OFF

Why It's Bad: Nothing happens. No repricing occurs.

Fix: After configuring, remember to toggle enabled = ON to activate repricing.

Mistake #7: Using Same Settings for All Products

Problem: Copy-pasting identical settings across completely different products

Why It's Bad: Premium products need different strategy than commodities. One size doesn't fit all.

Fix: Customize settings per product based on:

  • Margin requirements
  • Competition level
  • Sales velocity
  • Strategic importance

Mistake #8: Too Narrow Handling Time Filter

Problem: Setting handling_time_group = FAST_SHIPPING when you don't actually ship that fast

Why It's Bad: You might have no competitors to compare against, or you're lying about shipping speed.

Fix: Set handling time to match YOUR actual fulfillment speed, not aspirational speed.

Mistake #9: Floor Higher Than Max

Problem: Setting floor_price = $100 and max_price = $90

Why It's Bad: Impossible situation. No valid price exists between floor and max.

Fix: Ensure max_price > floor_price with meaningful gap. Example: floor = $50, max = $150

Mistake #10: Ignoring Inventory Thresholds

Problem: Setting own_vendor_threshold = 1 when you frequently run low on stock

Why It's Bad: You'll aggressively compete and sell out your last unit at lowest price.

Fix: Set own_vendor_threshold = 5-10 to slow down sales when stock is low.

Troubleshooting Guide

Repricing not working as expected? Use this diagnostic guide.

Problem: Prices Aren't Changing At All

Check These Settings in Order:

  1. enabled = ON?

    Most common issue. Check the master toggle.

  2. own_vendor_threshold exceeded?

    If your stock is below threshold, repricing stops. Check current inventory.

  3. Cron jobs running?

    Repricing requires scheduled jobs to execute. Verify cron is active.

  4. Vendor excluded?

    Make sure your vendor ID isn't in exclude_vendors or inactive_vendor_id.

Problem: Prices Only Going Down, Never Up

Check:

  • up_down setting: Is it set to DOWN only? Change to UP/DOWN for flexibility.
  • Market conditions: If all competitors are dropping prices, algorithm follows.
  • max_price setting: Is it set too low? Algorithm can't go up if max is already reached.

Problem: Prices Stuck at Floor

Diagnosis:

This means the market price is below your profitable level.

Options:

  1. Accept it: You're protecting margins correctly. You won't win sales but won't lose money.
  2. Lower floor: If you calculated floor too high, adjust downward (carefully!).
  3. Exit market: If market is consistently below your floor, this product isn't profitable.
  4. Enable floor_compete_with_next: Match next competitor above floor instead of sitting at floor.

Problem: Competing With Wrong Sellers

Check These Filters:

Filter What to Check
badge_indicator Set to BADGE to only compete with verified sellers
handling_time_group Set to your fulfillment speed to filter slow shippers
inventory_competition_threshold Increase to ignore sellers with minimal stock
exclude_vendors Add IDs of bad actors or unreliable sellers

Problem: Prices Changing Too Frequently

Possible Causes:

  • Volatile market: Competitors changing prices constantly
  • Too sensitive percentages: Small percentage thresholds trigger frequent changes
  • Low inventory thresholds: Algorithm reacts to every competitor

Solutions:

  • Increase inventory_competition_threshold to ignore small sellers
  • Adjust reprice percentages to be less aggressive
  • Consider keep_position = ON for more stable positioning

Problem: Margins Too Low

Immediate Actions:

  1. Raise floor_price

    Increase to cost + desired minimum margin

  2. Reduce reprice_down_percentage

    Stop undercutting so aggressively (try 1-2% instead of 5-10%)

  3. Change up_down to UP only

    Temporarily stop lowering prices until margins recover

  4. Switch to badge_indicator = BADGE

    Only compete with premium sellers, avoid race to bottom

Problem: Not Winning Buy Box

Buy Box Factors Beyond Price:

  • Seller rating and feedback score
  • Shipping speed (faster wins)
  • Stock availability (more stock = better chance)
  • Return policy and seller performance

Algorithm Adjustments:

  • Set price_strategy = BUY_BOX (advanced)
  • Ensure handling_time_group matches your actual fast shipping
  • Increase own_vendor_threshold to ensure healthy stock levels
  • Consider slight undercut with reprice_down_percentage = 1-2%

Diagnostic Checklist

  1. ✓ enabled = ON

  4. ✓ floor_price < max_price with reasonable gap
